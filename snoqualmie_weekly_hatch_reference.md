# Snoqualmie River Weekly Hatch Reference

This reference maps the app's insect and stage combinations to likely fishing windows on the Snoqualmie River system, especially the forks above Snoqualmie Falls near North Bend. It is a practical planning guide, not a guarantee of a visible hatch on a specific day.

Use this with current flows, water temperature, weather, and WDFW regulations. The Snoqualmie forks are freestone Cascade streams; runoff, rain events, and cold water can shift activity by multiple weeks.

## How to Read This

- Weeks are approximate ISO-style calendar weeks.
- "Primary" means the combination is one of the first things to try.
- "Secondary" means plausible, but usually dependent on weather, water clarity, time of day, or observed bugs.
- Subsurface stages can work outside hatch windows because trout eat aquatic insects year-round.
- Adult/dry stages depend more heavily on actual surface activity.

## Combination Windows

| Insect | Stage | Primary weeks | Secondary weeks | When to choose it |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Caddis | Larva | 1-21, 44-52 | 22-43 | Good default subsurface choice when no hatch is visible. Especially useful in cold water, high-but-clear flows, or before evening activity. |
| Caddis | Pupa / Emerger | 18-39 | 14-17, 40-43 | Use when caddis are active, pupal shucks are present, or trout are taking just under the film. Often best late afternoon through dusk. |
| Caddis | Adult | 18-39 | 40-43 | Best during visible caddis flight, skittering, egg-laying, or evening rises. Strongest summer dry-fly category in this app. |
| Mayfly | Nymph | 1-52 | - | Always plausible. Use small mayfly nymphs as a baseline when fish are not rising. |
| Mayfly | Emerger | 9-17, 22-25, 36-43 | 18-21, 26-31 | Use during overcast spring/fall olive activity or when trout bulge without clean surface takes. |
| Mayfly | Dun | 9-17, 22-25, 36-43 | 18-21, 26-31 | Use when upright-winged mayflies are on the water and fish are taking on top. |
| Mayfly | Spinner | 22-31 | 18-21, 36-39 | Best in calm morning/evening spinner falls. Less reliable in broken pocket water. |
| Stonefly | Nymph | 1-52 | - | Always plausible in the forks' rocky water. Use as a searching nymph in faster runs and pocket water. |
| Stonefly | Adult | 22-31 | 18-21, 32-35 | Use only when larger stoneflies are visible, fish are looking up, or a buoyant dry/dropper makes sense in summer pocket water. |

## Field Rules for the Snoqualmie

- If the river is high, cold, or recently blown out: start with caddis larva, mayfly nymph, or stonefly nymph.
- If fish are rising but you cannot see bugs: try mayfly emerger in spring/fall, caddis pupa in summer evenings.
- If bugs are flying but fish are not rising: stay subsurface; adults in the air do not always mean adults on the water.
- If you see splashy evening rises: prioritize caddis adult or pupa.
- If you see quiet sipping rises: prioritize mayfly emerger, dun, or spinner.
- If fishing pocket water in summer: a buoyant stonefly adult can work as an attractor even when it is not a literal hatch.
