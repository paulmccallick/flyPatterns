# Fly Pattern Lifecycle Reference

This file summarizes fishing-relevant lifecycle stages and representative fly patterns for caddisflies, mayflies, and stoneflies. Pattern lists are intentionally limited to common Washington and Idaho trout patterns, with no more than four patterns per stage.

Method labels:

- Dry: fished on or in the surface film.
- Wet: fished below the surface, including nymphs, larvae, pupae, soft hackles, and subsurface emergers.
- Streamer: actively retrieved baitfish, leech, sculpin, or attractor-style subsurface flies. None of the selected top lifecycle imitations are streamers.

## Quick Comparison

| Insect group | Metamorphosis | Fishing-relevant stages shown | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Caddisfly | Complete | Larva, pupa / emerger, adult | Egg and egg-laying behavior are not shown as separate stages because they do not materially change the selected top patterns. |
| Mayfly | Incomplete, with two winged adult forms | Nymph, emerger, dun, spinner | Dun and spinner remain separate because anglers use different imitations for each. |
| Stonefly | Incomplete | Nymph, adult | Stoneflies have no pupal stage; migrating nymphs are collapsed into nymph because the same fly choices apply. |

## Caddisflies

Caddisflies are Trichoptera. Their larvae and pupae are aquatic, while adults are terrestrial or aerial. They have complete metamorphosis: egg, larva, pupa, adult. For this fishing reference, eggs are omitted and egg-laying adults are folded into adult because the same top dry-fly and soft-hackle choices generally cover the useful angling behavior.

### Larva

Larvae are aquatic and are the longest, most important feeding stage. Many larvae build portable cases from silk plus sand, gravel, plant fragments, sticks, or other stream material. Some species are free-living or build fixed retreats and filtering nets rather than portable cases. Larvae can drift in the current, crawl on the streambed, or remain in retreats.

Representative fly patterns:

| Pattern | Method |
| --- | --- |
| Green Rock Worm | Wet |
| Bead Head Caddis Larva | Wet |
| Cased Caddis | Wet |
| Walt's Worm | Wet |

### Pupa / Emerger

Mature larvae pupate underwater, often inside a case or silk cocoon. Developed pupae cut out of the case or cocoon, swim or rise toward the surface, and emerge into adults. This rising stage is important because the insect is exposed in the water column and surface film.

Representative fly patterns:

| Pattern | Method |
| --- | --- |
| LaFontaine Sparkle Pupa | Wet |
| Deep Sparkle Pupa | Wet |
| Soft Hackle Caddis | Wet |
| X-Caddis | Dry |

### Adult

Adults look moth-like, with tent-shaped hairy wings folded over the body. Many are most active in low light or at night. Adults mate, then females return to lay eggs. Some skitter, dive, crawl, or touch the water while egg-laying.

Representative fly patterns:

| Pattern | Method |
| --- | --- |
| Elk Hair Caddis | Dry |
| Henryville Special | Dry |
| X-Caddis | Dry |
| CDC & Elk | Dry |

## Mayflies

Mayflies are Ephemeroptera. They have incomplete metamorphosis, but unlike most insects they have two winged post-nymph stages. Fly anglers commonly call the subimago a dun and the sexually mature adult an imago or spinner. Eggs are omitted because they are not a useful separate fly-fishing stage.

### Nymph

Nymphs are aquatic and make up most of the mayfly lifespan. Nymph forms vary by habitat and behavior: clingers, crawlers, swimmers, and burrowers are common angling categories. Nymphs molt through multiple instars before emergence.

Representative fly patterns:

| Pattern | Method |
| --- | --- |
| Pheasant Tail Nymph | Wet |
| Gold Ribbed Hare's Ear | Wet |
| Copper John | Wet |
| Baetis Nymph | Wet |

### Emerger

Mature nymphs move toward the surface, swim up, drift upward, or crawl out depending on species. The insect transitions from aquatic nymph to winged dun. This stage is vulnerable because the insect may be trapped in or just below the surface film.

Representative fly patterns:

| Pattern | Method |
| --- | --- |
| RS2 | Wet |
| WD-40 | Wet |
| Barr's Emerger | Wet |
| Sparkle Dun | Dry |

Sparkle Dun is kept with emergers because it imitates the emerger-to-dun transition with a trailing shuck, even though it is fished as a dry fly.

### Dun

The dun is a winged, usually sexually immature stage. Duns often have duller bodies and cloudier wings than spinners. This stage is short-lived, often less than a day, before molting again.

Representative fly patterns:

| Pattern | Method |
| --- | --- |
| Parachute Adams | Dry |
| Comparadun | Dry |
| Pale Morning Dun | Dry |
| Blue Wing Olive | Dry |

### Spinner

The spinner is the sexually mature adult. Adults mate in swarms, then females return to the water to lay eggs. After egg-laying, dead or dying spinners may lie spent on the water with wings outstretched.

Representative fly patterns:

| Pattern | Method |
| --- | --- |
| Rusty Spinner | Dry |
| Trico Spinner | Dry |
| Blue Wing Olive Spinner | Dry |
| Pale Morning Dun Spinner | Dry |

## Stoneflies

Stoneflies are Plecoptera. They have incomplete metamorphosis: egg, nymph, adult. There is no pupal stage. The aquatic nymph is usually the most important subsurface stage, while the adult stage can be important during large seasonal emergences. Eggs are omitted because they are not a useful separate fly-fishing stage.

### Nymph

Nymphs are aquatic and live on or near the streambed, often in well-oxygenated streams and rivers. They resemble wingless adults and may take one or more years to mature. Mature nymphs usually crawl out of the water onto rocks, logs, banks, bridge pilings, or shoreline vegetation before becoming adults. This migration is still represented by the nymph stage because the same patterns are used.

Representative fly patterns:

| Pattern | Method |
| --- | --- |
| Pat's Rubber Legs | Wet |
| Girdle Bug | Wet |
| Kaufmann's Stone | Wet |
| Bitch Creek Nymph | Wet |

### Adult

Adults have two pairs of wings that usually lie flat over the back. Many adults are weak fliers and stay near the stream or river where they emerged. Females return to water to drop or dab eggs, and wind or clumsy flight can knock large adults onto the water.

Representative fly patterns:

| Pattern | Method |
| --- | --- |
| Chubby Chernobyl | Dry |
| Sofa Pillow | Dry |
| Parks' Salmonfly | Dry |
| Rogue Foam Stone | Dry |

## Cross-Insect Notes

- Emerger means different things by group:
  - Caddis: aquatic pupa swimming or rising to the surface and transforming into an adult.
  - Mayfly: nymph transforming into a dun at or near the surface, or after crawling out in some species.
  - Stonefly: nymph crawling out of the water and splitting its shuck on a fixed surface.
- Adult should not be treated as one generic state:
  - Caddis adults are moth-like, tent-winged, and often active in low light.
  - Mayfly duns and spinners are visually and behaviorally different.
  - Stonefly adults hold wings flat over the body and often remain near shore.
